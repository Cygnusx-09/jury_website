Shawls

In the high Himalayas, Pashmina begins as an ultra-fine inner coat on Changthangi goats living at altitudes around Ladakh, evolved to survive winters that drop well below freezing. In the valley of Kashmir, that fibre is cleaned, hand-spun on simple wheels and woven on wooden looms into shawls that have wrapped royalty and travellers in “soft gold” for centuries. Pashmina is a small window into this craft, focusing on the people, processes and quiet labour behind each piece rather than just the product.

What is Pashmina?

Pashmina comes from the word “pashm” – a term with Persian and Urdu roots that refers to the fine under-fleece of mountain goats, not general wool. In Kashmir, that fibre is transformed into extremely light, warm shawls that became symbols of status in royal courts and among European elites from at least the 15th century onwards.

Unlike regular wool or commercial cashmere, Kashmiri Pashmina uses very fine fibres (around 12–16 microns) that are hand-spun and hand-woven, which is why a pure piece feels weightless yet incredibly warm. Because this quality is tied closely to place and technique, “Kashmir Pashmina” is protected with a Geographical Indication (GI) tag in India.
